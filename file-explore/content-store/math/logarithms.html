Logarithms and Exponentials

Way of Looking at Exponentials

Exponentials are like a "growth machine" (Expansion).

  • General Form: \(a^n\)
    • \(a\) is the base (like the growth rate)
    • \(n\) is the exponent (like time)
  • Special Cases:
    • \(2^0 = 1\): Like "no growth" (100% no change). The result stays the same regardless of time growth.
    • Negative Growth: \(2^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}\). This represents the inverse of the growth process.
  • Example: \(2^5\) is a "doubling machine" function.

Way of Looking at Logarithms

Logarithms are essentially a "shuffling of terms" used to solve for time.

  • General Form: \(\log_{\text{base}}(\text{result}) = \text{exponent}\)
  • Examples:
    • \(\log_{2} 32 = 5\): "Log base 2 of 32 equals 5."
    • \(\log_{2} 1 = 0\): At a growth rate of 2, a result of 1 means we grew for \(0\) time.
    • \(\log_{2} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = -1\)
    • \(\log_{3} 81 = 4\): Represents 4 "rotations" in the "tripling machine" (\(3^4 = 81\)).

Practice Problem

Solve: \(\log_{\sqrt{3}} 27 = ?\)

  1. Identity: \(\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3} = 3\) (which is \((\sqrt{3})^2 = 3\))
  2. Goal: Find \(n\) such that \((\sqrt{3})^n = 27\)
  3. Substitution: Since \(3^3 = 27\) and \(3 = (\sqrt{3})^2\):
    \[((\sqrt{3})^2)^3 = 27\]
    \[(\sqrt{3})^6 = 27\]
    Result: \(6\)
